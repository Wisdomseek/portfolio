Philosophy

Projects & Assignments (Psychology of Personality— Psy 301)

Personality Project

For this project in Psychology of Personality, I designed and attempted a six-week behavioral intervention aimed at increasing my conscientiousness, which initially tested at about the 4th percentile on the Big Five inventory. I created a system of weekly challenges focused on academic follow-through: attending every class I could, arriving on time with materials, and handwriting 100–150 word mini-reflections within six hours after each class. My plan was rooted in current personality-change research done by Hudson et al. (2019), who suggested that meaningful trait change depends on consistently enacting trait-relevant behaviors over time (not just willpower).

Factor Analysis

In this project, I was challenged to use an exploratory factor analysis to see whether a short personality survey would recover the classic Big Five traits from item-level data. I ran a Maximum Likelihood factor analysis with Promax rotation, suppressing loadings below .30 to focus on the strongest relationships between items and factors. The analysis showed a five-factor solution: each set of five items clustered significantly on one factor with cross-loadings that were minimal, which came in close approximation with Extraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Neuroticism, and Openness to Experience. By interpreting each factor in its highest-loading items (for example, "talkative," "energetic," and "outgoing" for Extraversion; "sympathetic," "helpful," and "kind" for Agreeableness). I learned that abstract traits can emerge from concrete self-descriptions.

Jamovi Project 2: t-tests

In this project, we learned through what measures inferential Stats can be done. I learned here that a t-test is crucial when attempting to make claims about the unknown (which lots of us do with our feelings all the time). This was my output: "A one-sample t test was conducted to compare the sample mean test score to the population mean of 69. The results were statistically significant, t(df) = 2.06, p = .048, with a mean difference of 5.35, indicating that the sample mean test score was significantly higher than the population mean when the outlier was included."

Jamovi Project 3: ANOVA

This project taught me the crucial necessity that your groups have as little variability as possible when trying to compare them to ultimately make a claim. Imagine if you had two groups where one was completely different from the other in the sense of what you are trying to measure... you are in big trouble. But that's what ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) is here for. Here's the output that I got: "Ho: All three teaching methods have equal mean ARGS scores. Ha: At least one group differs in mean ARGS. A one-way ANOVA showed no significant effect of teaching method on reading comprehension, F(2,63)=0.45, p = .64, n2 = .01. Homogeneity of variance was met (Levene's test p = .18). Bonferroni post-hoc tests indicated that the Basal, Directed, and Strategies groups did not differ significantly from one another in mean ARGS scores."

Jamovi Project 4: Correlation & Regression

This is perhaps one of the most important lessons (that I learned) in all of Statistics, and I'm sure you've heard it being said before: "Correlation is not Causation". But what does this really mean? Simply put, it's always easily possible to use the power of our fertile imaginations to draw a connection/relationship between two things that may be (and often are) very unrelated. Correlation and Regression standardize what is a strong vs weak relationship between one or more variables— and it takes confounds into account so that researchers might not be misled to make claims that aren't true. Here's my output: "A Pearson correlation indicated that medication dosage and heart rate were not significantly associated, r(148)=.04, p=.65, suggesting essentially no linear relationship between dosage level and heart rate in this sample. A simple linear regression with heart rate as the outcome and dosage (coded 0 = low, 1 = medium, 2 = high) as the predictor showed that the overall model was not statistically significant, F(1,148)=0.20, p=.65, and explained virtually none of the variance in heart rate, adjusted R2 = 0. The unstandardized regression coefficient indicated that each one-level increase in dosage was associated with only a 0.54 bpm increase in predicted heart rate, b = 0.54; p=.65, which is a trivial and non-significant effect. Together, these results suggest that in this dataset, higher medication dosages are not meaningfully related to higher or lower heart rates."

Mind map of the whole Statistics course

My task was to do a mind map on the whole semester. It was so challenging but also very fun to better picture how everything connects. Descriptive stats allow for Prediction and Inference to happen. One cannot reliably exist without the other— since to do stats in the first place we have to assume normality (the famous bell curve).

Chi Square Method of Hypothesis

A statistical formula we use when trying to make claims of how two categorical variables relate to each other, both in the present and future. Fun stuff like "does someone who eats chocolate vs vanilla ice cream before an exam get a better score?". In this one, I made it about soccer (of course) and came to the conclusion that simply being of Brazilian nationality makes you statistically more likely to be better at it than someone born in the U.S. Here's my result: "A chi-square test of independence examined the relation between nationality (Brazil vs. USA) and soccer skill (good vs. not good). The association was statistically significant, chi-square(1, N = 2200) = 5.01, p < .05, indicating that nationality and soccer skill were not independent. Specifically, Brazil had more good players than expected by chance (300 observed vs. 272.7 expected), whereas the USA had fewer good players than expected (300 observed vs. 327.3 expected)."

End of Courses Reflections

Writing Sample I'm Most Proud Of (Personality)

"The constructs we hold fundamentally shift how we experience ourselves and others around us. In a compensatory reflex toward self protection (what can also be called the EGO) we merge ourselves with extremes; even if the behavior is counterproductive. The major takeaway for me is that in order to become a more loving being, we ought to practice thinking of constructs (people, circumstances) in a continuous spectrum rather than in opposite, fixed states (Good, bad; Fair, unfair). In this way, I believe we ought to optimize our environments so that they can produce in us heuristics that yield positive results at the micro level (interpersonally) and the macro level (society as a whole)."

Expectations Vs. Reality (Personality)

Frankly speaking, I did not expect much from it, as I had already taken a different version with a different professor before. I expected it to be fairly familiar conceptually, but I couldn't have been more wrong. Dr. Xeriland introduced various concepts borrowed from Physics (yes, you read that right) in order for us to have a framework to understand personality (such as Chaos Theory). It was a bit stumping at first, but as I got accustomed to the different definitions, it allowed me to look at human personality through a lense I had never seen before.

Borrowing from Chaos Theory, we learned how personality can be looked at as both a fixed and fluid state, ever ebbing between those two poles. What we assume about the "predictable ways" that we act has more to do with our tendency to project order in a chaotic world (to cope, of course), than with any sense of an objective pattern, such as those found in the field of Statistics. What I loved about this course was that in many ways, it reminded me how both complex and simple people are. The more nuance the better. One of the most challenging parts of the course was the initial overwhelm from terms I had never heard of. At the same time, that was also the most rewarding part of the course: coming away with new frameworks that will never leave me.

Thanks to this course, I plan to use some of the later personality theories on behavior/motivation to experiment and improve my life across different goals that I have. Skinner's concept of Negative Reinforcement (being pushed to do something because you want to avoid losing something else) stands out to me as a powerful tool I learned about in this course that I can apply toward my objectives.

Final Remarks (Personality)

One of the core advantages of studying personality is that being exposed to different ways of framing human behavior carries an inherent lesson on humility and compassion.

Humility; because to address any change in behavior in a highly westernized, idolizing culture of success that we are inserted in, requires sacrificing a degree of self-protection, expressed as the ego. It requires a commitment to give up a part of oneself for the greater best. And I think anytime that we come comfort-seeking humans are pushed to do so, something good happens.

Compassion; because when you're constantly allowing yourself to be exposed to your own vulnerabilities, you soon start noticing that other people have vulnerabilities as well, and that they shouldn't be judged for those things. And that you understand; you know what it's like to be flawed (because you had the courage to face yourself), you know what it's like to not know, you know what it's like to be ignorant, you know what it's like to be demotivated, and that allows you to go past your initial reaction to seeing something in someone else that might make you feel judgmental. It allows you to actually pause and think: how can you help build that person up truly?

So that's why I believe learning different frameworks of personality, especially in the way that this class presented it, can be so, so powerful.

Expectation vs Reality (Statistics)

My expectations coming into the class were that I'd be taught theorems and formulas all over again (I have taken this class and failed before), the same old same old. But what I actually got was an instructor who was committed on teaching the fundamental concepts of Statistics, not that we just memorize information and move along without LEARNING anything. Having such commitment be displayed on the classroom was by far the most satisfying part of the course. The most challenging part of it is the homework. It's not fun to do the same calculations 15 different times within the span of 40 minutes.

How my Mind was Changed (Statistics)

My view of Statistics in the beginning of the semester had been that it is an amazing tool for finding out the "truth". When I shared that thought to Dr. Xeriland (my professor), he quickly shut down the idea. He explained how you can never truly know the full picture, and that's why we have stats in the first place. So I've changed my mind to say that Stats is an excellent tool to achieve the most optimal level of clarity and ACCURACY when facing uncertainty about something bigger than what you can grasp.

Final Remarks (Statistics)

Statistics is an incredible framework for anchoring yourself in a world that is constantly making claims about EVERYTHING. As humans, we are incessantly curious— and so we ask questions, and it would be great if we stopped there. But no— we are also naturally prolific at jumping to conclusions that reflect what our biases want to hear. And in a world where everyone is hallucinating their reality based on how they feel, how they were raised, what they believe... Statistical thinking provides an enormous advantage; the skill of being sober minded when making critical decisions based on what could most likely happen. It's truly fascinating!
