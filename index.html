What  learning  means  to  me

Learning & Growth

Learning is about growth. It is about destroying one's fundamental constructs about the world and rebuilding them back with stronger foundations, incessantly. It is to do what you can to remain intellectually honest, even when your ego does not want to. It requires a mindset of growth over scarcity—and it inherently carries a lesson worth taking anywhere: Vulnerability is a strength that gets you to where you want to be.

One does not learn without being vulnerable; without facing how much they do not know. Those who cling to the external image that they must have all the answers learn nothing—and in not learning, they do not grow.

Statistics & Personality

Statistics, like any other discipline, equips an individual with a better heuristic than the one they were born with. Specifically, it involves various skills including (but not limited to): Critical Thinking, Interpretation of data, Working with uncertainty (which helps if you don't want to be driven by emotions). In addition, a framework for understanding human personality is fundamental to understand the microcosm of the self.

In doing so, we (hopefully) become better capable to love ourselves and byproduct those around us. I've learned that whereas statistics allows us to peek into the macrocosm with predictions, the study of personality humbles us to think back to the basics of what it means to function as a human, imperfectly.

Coursework

Projects & Assignments (Psychology of Personality — Psy 301)

Personality Project. For this project in Psychology of Personality, I designed and attempted a six-week behavioral intervention aimed at increasing my conscientiousness, which initially tested at about the 4th percentile on the Big Five inventory. I created a system of weekly challenges focused on academic follow-through: attending every class I could, arriving on time with materials, and handwriting 100–150 word mini-reflections within six hours after each class. My plan was rooted in current personality-change research done by Hudson et al. (2019), who suggested that meaningful trait change depends on consistently enacting trait-relevant behaviors over time (not just willpower).

Factor Analysis

In this project, I was challenged to use an exploratory factor analysis to see whether a short personality survey would recover the classic Big Five traits from item-level data. I ran a Maximum Likelihood factor analysis with Promax rotation, suppressing loadings below .30 to focus on the strongest relationships between items and factors. The analysis showed a five-factor solution that closely approximated Extraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Neuroticism, and Openness to Experience. I learned that abstract traits can emerge from concrete self-descriptions.

Jamovi Projects (Statistics — Psy 309)

Project 1: Data exploration and descriptive statistics. I learned about the importance of visualizing data and summarizing it through measures of central tendency. For example, I reported on MLU (Mean Length of Utterance) for 20 patients and described the distribution in terms of mean, SD, range, and modal values.

Project 2: t-tests. I explored how inferential statistics can be used to make claims about the unknown. Using a one-sample t test, I compared a sample mean test score to a population mean and interpreted the significance of the difference when an outlier was included.

Project 3: ANOVA. I learned why controlling variability within groups is crucial when comparing them. A one-way ANOVA tested the effect of teaching method on reading comprehension, checking assumptions like homogeneity of variance and interpreting post-hoc tests.

Project 4: Correlation & Regression. I engaged deeply with the idea that “correlation is not causation.” I used Pearson correlation and simple linear regression to test relationships between medication dosage and heart rate, interpreting effect sizes, p-values, and explained variance.

Chi-Square & Mindmap

Chi Square Method of Hypothesis. I used chi-square to examine how two categorical variables relate, framing it around soccer and nationality. I found that being Brazilian vs. American was statistically associated with soccer skill, illustrating how categorical relationships can be tested.

I also built a mind map for the whole Statistics course, connecting how descriptive statistics support prediction and inference. It helped me visualize how the pieces of the course fit together into a coherent framework.

Reflections

Writing Sample I'm Most Proud Of (Personality)

“The constructs we hold fundamentally shift how we experience ourselves and others around us. In a compensatory reflex toward self protection (what can also be called the EGO) we merge ourselves with extremes; even if the behavior is counterproductive. The major takeaway for me is that in order to become a more loving being, we ought to practice thinking of constructs (people, circumstances) in a continuous spectrum rather than in opposite, fixed states (Good, bad; Fair, unfair). In this way, I believe we ought to optimize our environments so that they can produce in us heuristics that yield positive results at the micro level (interpersonally) and the macro level (society as a whole).”

Expectations vs. Reality (Personality)

Frankly speaking, I did not expect much from it, as I had already taken a different version with a different professor before. I expected it to be fairly familiar conceptually, but I couldn't have been more wrong. Dr. Xeriland introduced various concepts borrowed from Physics in order for us to have a framework to understand personality (such as Chaos Theory). It was a bit stumping at first, but as I got accustomed to the different definitions, it allowed me to look at human personality through a lens I had never seen before.

Borrowing from Chaos Theory, we learned how personality can be looked at as both a fixed and fluid state, ever ebbing between those two poles. What we assume about the “predictable ways” that we act has more to do with our tendency to project order in a chaotic world than with any sense of an objective pattern. This course reminded me how both complex and simple people are; the more nuance the better.

Thanks to this course, I plan to use some of the later personality theories on behavior/motivation to experiment and improve my life across different goals that I have. Skinner's concept of Negative Reinforcement stands out to me as a powerful tool I can apply toward my objectives.

Final Remarks (Personality)

One of the core advantages of studying personality is that being exposed to different ways of framing human behavior carries an inherent lesson on humility and compassion.

Humility; because to address any change in behavior in a highly westernized, idolizing culture of success, requires sacrificing a degree of self-protection, expressed as the ego. It requires a commitment to give up a part of oneself for the greater best.

Compassion; because when you're constantly allowing yourself to be exposed to your own vulnerabilities, you soon start noticing that other people have vulnerabilities as well, and that they shouldn't be judged for those things. That awareness lets you pause, think, and ask how you can help build that person up truly.

Expectation vs Reality (Statistics)

My expectations coming into the class were that I'd be taught theorems and formulas all over again (I have taken this class and failed before), the same old same old. But what I actually got was an instructor who was committed to teaching the fundamental concepts of Statistics, not that we just memorize information and move along without learning anything. Having such commitment displayed in the classroom was by far the most satisfying part of the course. The most challenging part of it was the homework.

How my Mind was Changed (Statistics)

My view of Statistics in the beginning of the semester had been that it is an amazing tool for finding out the “truth”. When I shared that thought to Dr. Xeriland, he quickly shut down the idea. He explained how you can never truly know the full picture, and that's why we have stats in the first place. So I've changed my mind to say that Stats is an excellent tool to achieve the most optimal level of clarity and accuracy when facing uncertainty about something bigger than what you can grasp.

Final Remarks (Statistics)

Statistics is an incredible framework for anchoring yourself in a world that is constantly making claims about everything. As humans, we are incessantly curious—and so we ask questions, and it would be great if we stopped there. But we are also naturally prolific at jumping to conclusions that reflect what our biases want to hear. Statistical thinking provides an enormous advantage; the skill of being sober minded when making critical decisions based on what could most likely happen.
